Fall from Glory

viewing Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep? and The Windup Girl through a historical lens

Written in 1968

Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep? is a dystopian novel that reflects a number of fears in the 1960s.

Concepts such as the race to nuclear armament and to space feature heavily in the book, and new technologies such as humanoid robots are also touched upon.

Dystopic attributes are also shown throughout the book, with characters living in isolation, poor living conditions, and the Earth becoming a nuclear wasteland.

Overall, the book discusses the fears associated with the potentially catastrophic outcomes of using nuclear weapons and the development of new technology, which were major concerns when the book was written during the midst of the Cold War.

Written in 2009

The Windup Girl discusses topics such as the extreme of effects of climate change, with flooding and extreme heat, strains of medicine-immune diseases that can cause famine and pandemics, and the ethics of using biotechnology.

The society in this book also clearly has dystopic attributes, with very low living standards, political instability, and repeated examples of large-scale corruption.

As well, Emiko, a human-machine hybrid created through genetical engineering, is exploited and humiliated by a sex-trafficking organization, raising questions on whether bioengineered beings deserve the same rights as actual human beings.

Similar to Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?, The Windup Girl discusses a number of concerns relevant to the era, including those relating to climate change, diseases, and technological ethics.

The Connnection

Although Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep? and The Windup Girl were written in completely different eras, the books share a number of more profound similarities.

Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep? focuses on a number of topics that are directly related to the circumstances of society when the book was written, such as nuclear war and armament, the race to space, and the development of new technology.

The Windup Girl does the same, exploring subjects that are more relevant to the 21st century, such as climate change, uncurable diseases, and developing biotechnology and its ethicality.

As is shown, both books take topics, as well as fears related to them, that define the era that they were written in, and build a society using them, creating a novel that is not only engaging and though-provoking, but highly relevant to the readers as well.
